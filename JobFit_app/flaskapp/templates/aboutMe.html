About Me

I am an analytical thinker who loves to tackle complex problems using a variety of techniques. Data science has provided the perfect playground to explore complicated real world problems and I want to combine my keen research ability with the fast-paced field of data science as I progress in my career.

I recently graduated with a Ph.D. from the Department of Physics at the University of Toronto with a focus on experimental quantum optics. I previously completed a M.Sc. in Physics at the University of Toronto and a B.Sc. (hons) in the Physics Department at the University of British Columbia with a senior thesis in high energy physics.

Outside of my academic experience I have cultivated a diverse teaching record with a focus on social justice and equity. Through my teaching and volunteer work I encourage equitable and accessible participation for individuals with all kinds of backgrounds and experience.

Some selected projects I have worked on are listed below:

Predicting Successful TED Talks

This project involves utilizing collated data on the number of views a TED talk has received to determine the key parameters that result in a widely shared and viewed educational video. This analysis extends to other genres of videos as long as they are assigned similar attributes.

Analysis of TED Talk Content

Understanding what makes a good presentation is the goal of many individuals in business meetings to academic conferences. Using the transcripts of successful (and unsuccessful) TED talks I statistically analyse what patterns in speech make for highly viewed content.

More Info »

Hedging Securities to Minimize Risk

In finance, hedging is the practice of purchasing an investment to minimize the risk in holding another investment. In this project I build a simple model that builds a proxy basket of four securities to be hedged such that they track the performance of a target instrument. This algorithm is fast and flexible and can be deployed on varied sets of time series data.

More Info »

Neural Networks for Image Recognition

Fast classification of images is essential in an ultracold atom experiment. This project shows the development of an algorithm that classifies the temperature of an ultracold atom cloud using a neural network to vastly improve the speed of image classification in comparison to a non-linear fitting routine.
